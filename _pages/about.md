Hi there! I’m Aaron, a cognitive scientist interested in how humans think about and navigate the social world across the lifespan. I’m currently a postdoc at Havard, where I’m working with Ashley Thomas. Before that, I completed my PhD at Stanford, where I primarily worked with Hyowon Gweon. Outside of research, I enjoy cooking, surfing, perusing record stores, and collecting tattoos. 

What do I study?
======
The human mind is not an island. From birth, people’s knowledge, thoughts, feelings, goals, and behaviors are shaped by others. By that same token, explaining why people act the way they do and predicting what people will do in the future requires us to understand how social influence works. Furthermore, humans occupy a complex social world composed of various relationships, groups, and institutions that determine who interacts with whom and how in practice. How do humans think about the structure and dynamics of their social environment? 

I examine this question by studying how humans, across the lifespan, reason about the mental states of people embedded in various social interactions or structures. I am particularly interested in how children think about information transmission, including during real-time exchanges they can directly observe, unobserved exchanges that may have happened, as well as indirect exchanges that result from a chain of communicative interactions, like in games of “Telephone”. I am also interested in how children represent social networks and the extent to which they can utilize these representations to reason about the flow of information within them.

How do I study it?
======
My research uses simple, yet powerful behavioral experiments with infants, children, and adults. I try to design studies that both examine cognition in the context in which it naturally occurs and are well controlled. In short, I want to “capture” phenomena of interest in a satisfying way while also licensing causal explanations about how humans think. This could mean measuring children’s surprise in real-time conversations, teaching strategies during a structured situation, or even who they say is friends with whom in their preschool class. I am particularly fond of online studies, which enable researchers to rabidly collect high-quality data from large samples of infants and children. My research is also deeply informed by work in computational cognitive science, particularly probabilistic models like Bayesian Theory of Mind, Rational Pedagogy, and Rational Speech Act that treat mental-state reasoning as a core component of social cognition and behavior.
